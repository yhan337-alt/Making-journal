# Data Physicalisation Project – Invisible Participation

## In-Class Activities

## 1. Data Exploration

### 1.1 Data source and where it comes from

This project uses original qualitative data collected through a Google Forms survey. The participants are university students, especially international and multilingual students. Instead of using an existing dataset, I am generating new data based on lived experiences of communication, silence, hesitation, and emotional expression in social and educational environments.

Participants are asked to reflect on moments when they wanted to speak but stayed silent and describe the emotions they experienced in those moments. They are also asked to translate emotions into tactile descriptions (e.g., “If this emotion were a physical texture, what would it feel like?”). This allows emotional experiences to be treated as sensory data.

All responses are self-reported, meaning the data reflects personal memory and interpretation rather than objective measurement. The purpose is to understand silence not as absence, but as a meaningful form of participation.

https://docs.google.com/forms/d/e/1FAIpQLScAG5ZGfqNMFd6py6UuLPzl3GByW6cVff_1xJmFDg6OFGS_QQ/viewform?usp=dialog

### 1.2 What the data contains and how it is structured

The dataset contains short written responses describing emotional and social experiences.

**Key questions include:**
- Have you ever stayed silent when you wanted to speak?
- What emotion did you feel most strongly?
- How would you describe the moment in one sentence?
- What does that emotion feel like physically or tactually?

The data is qualitative (non-numerical) and organised into themes:
- Emotional responses (anxiety, embarrassment, loneliness, frustration)
- Communication barriers
- Silence and hesitation experiences
- Belonging and participation
- Tactile interpretations of emotions

As more responses are collected, patterns of “invisible participation” begin to emerge.

---

### 1.3 Limitations, biases, and gaps

A key limitation is self-reporting. Participants interpret and remember experiences differently, making the data subjective rather than factual.

Participation is voluntary, which may over-represent people who strongly relate to silence or communication difficulty.

Cultural and linguistic differences also affect interpretation of silence, making comparison complex.

Written responses cannot fully capture bodily or non-verbal emotional experiences.

However, these limitations are not treated as problems to fix. Instead, they become part of the design material. The ambiguity and incompleteness of the data reflect the nature of invisible participation.

## 3. Project Planning and Skills Roadmap

### 3.1 What I need to build

This project consists of two interconnected participatory systems:

#### 1. Sensory Black Box Interface

Participants interact with a black box by placing their hands inside it.

The black box functions as a sensory interface symbolising internal emotional states and silence.

Participants wear headphones and experience:
- Heartbeat audio
- Breathing sounds
- Low-frequency vibrations

Inside the box, different materials (rough, soft, cold, vibrating) simulate emotional states such as tension and anxiety.

This system expresses silence through sensory experience rather than visual representation.

---

#### 2. Transparent Accumulation System

After the sensory experience, participants respond to reflection questions about silence.

Each response is translated into a physical object (e.g., beads) and placed into a transparent container.

Over time, this creates a visible archive of silence.

**System flow:**
Sensory experience → emotional reflection → physical accumulation

---

## Independent Study

### 1. Consultation Reflection

The tutor noted that the project was initially too complex, with multiple ideas competing.

The AI + communication decline link was underdeveloped, weakening clarity.

Key feedback question:  
**“What do you want the audience to feel?”**

This led to a shift in focus:
- AI removed from concept
- Focus narrowed to “invisible participation”
- Silence reframed as participatory data, not absence

This was a turning point in simplifying the system and strengthening conceptual clarity.

---

### 2. Technical Skill Development

The first challenge was understanding how to transform qualitative emotional data into physical systems.

Research focused on:
- Data physicalisation systems
- Accumulation-based installations
- Interaction-based emotional mapping

Key learning:
The most important factor is not technological complexity, but the clarity of the relationship between action and outcome.

This confirmed that physical, material-based interaction is more suitable than purely digital systems.

---

### 3. Initial Concept Sketch

The initial sketch represents a participatory installation that visualises silence as physical data.

It is not a final design, but a prototype system for testing how emotional data can be translated into physical form.
